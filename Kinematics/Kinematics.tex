\section{Kinematics}
	\subsection{Key Terms}
		\begin{description}
			\item[Acceleration:]
				A change in velocity over time.
			
			\item[Velocity:] 
				A change in position over time, or the result of accumulated acceleration.
			
			\item[Position:]
			 	Where the particle is, or the result of accumulated velocity.
		\end{description}
		
	\subsection{Basic Formulas}
		\begin{enumerate}
			\item $x(t) = \frac{1}{2}at^2 + v_0t+x_0$
			\item $v(t)=at+v_0$
			\item $a(t) = a_0$
		\end{enumerate}	
	
	\subsection{Change Formulas} 
		Defining $\Delta A = A - A_0$, meaning that the change in $A$ is the difference of the variable $A$ and the initial $A$ when $t = 0$ (or simply $A_0$).
			\begin{enumerate}
				\item $\Delta x(t) = \frac{1}{2}at^2 + v_0t$
				\item $\Delta v(t)=at$
				\item $\Delta a(t) = 0$	
			\end{enumerate}
		Notice how the only difference is that the constant terms disappeared because they are absorbed into the $\Delta$ notation, which represents the change of that variable. 
	
	\subsection{Extended Formulas}
		\[v^2 = v_0^2+2a\Delta x\]
		\[x=x_0+\frac{(v_0+v)}{2}t\]
	
	\subsection{Understanding the Concept}
		To understand the relationship quantitatively, we will show the relation between $v$, $a$, and $x$ and derive their relation from the base.
		Assuming the only formula given is:
		\[a(t) = a_0\]
		
		\subsubsection*{From $a$ to $v$}
		The area under the graph of $a$ vs.\ $t$ is strictly a rectangle. Therefore the term $at$ represents the accumulation of acceleration over time $t$.
		\begin{center}
		\begin{tikzpicture}
			\begin{axis}[
				width=8cm, height=5cm,
				xlabel={$t$}, ylabel={$a$},
				axis lines=middle,
				xmin=0, xmax=5, ymin=0, ymax=3,
				xtick={4}, xticklabels={$t$},
				ytick={2}, yticklabels={$a_0$},
				clip=false,
			]
			% Shaded rectangle for area = a * t
			\addplot[fill=black!10, draw=none, domain=0:4] {2} \closedcycle;
			% Horizontal line a(t) = a_0
			\addplot[thick, black, domain=0:4.8] {2};
			% Dashed guides
			\draw[dashed, black] (axis cs:4,0) -- (axis cs:4,2);
			% Area label
			\node at (axis cs:2,1) {Area $= a_0 \cdot t$};
			\end{axis}
		\end{tikzpicture}
		\end{center}
		This gives us formula 2. Adding the constant term $v_0$ (the initial velocity), we obtain:
		\[v(t) = at + v_0\]
		
		\subsubsection*{From $v$ to $x$}
		Given $v(t) = at+v_0$, the graph of $v$ vs.\ $t$ is a straight line. The area under this line is a trapezoid, which we can divide into a rectangle and a triangle:
		\begin{center}
		\begin{tikzpicture}
			\begin{axis}[
				width=8cm, height=5.5cm,
				xlabel={$t$}, ylabel={$v$},
				axis lines=middle,
				xmin=0, xmax=5, ymin=0, ymax=5,
				xtick={4}, xticklabels={$t$},
				ytick={1.5, 4.5}, yticklabels={$v_0$, $v_0 + at$},
				clip=false,
			]
			% Rectangle (v_0 * t)
			\addplot[fill=black!10, draw=none] coordinates {(0,0) (4,0) (4,1.5) (0,1.5)} \closedcycle;
			% Triangle (1/2 * a * t^2)
			\addplot[pattern=north east lines, pattern color=black!40, draw=none] coordinates {(0,1.5) (4,1.5) (4,4.5)} \closedcycle;
			% v(t) line
			\addplot[thick, black, domain=0:4.5] {1.5 + 0.75*x};
			% Dashed guides
			\draw[dashed, black] (axis cs:4,0) -- (axis cs:4,4.5);
			\draw[dashed, black] (axis cs:0,1.5) -- (axis cs:4,1.5);
			% Area labels
			\node at (axis cs:2,0.7) {$v_0 t$};
			\node at (axis cs:2.5,2.7) {$\frac{1}{2}at^2$};
			\end{axis}
		\end{tikzpicture}
		\end{center}
		\[\text{Area of rectangle} = v_0 t\]
		\[\text{Area of triangle} = \frac{1}{2} a t^2\]
		Summing these gives formula 1. Adding the constant term $x_0$ (the initial position):
		\[x(t) = \frac{1}{2}at^2 + v_0 t + x_0\]
		
		Notice that the rectangle comes from the initial velocity, and the triangle accounts for the acceleration. If the acceleration is $0$, the triangle has zero area and the $v$ vs.\ $t$ graph is a horizontal line. If the initial velocity is $0$, the graph is a triangle starting from the origin, and the position equation reduces to $x(t) = \frac{1}{2} a t^2$.
				
		\subsubsection*{From $x$ back to $v$ and $a$}
		Now we start from the position formula and work backwards to show that for a parabolic graph, the acceleration is constant.
		Given $x(t) = \frac{1}{2}at^2 + v_0t+x_0$:
		\begin{center}
		\begin{tikzpicture}
			\begin{axis}[
				width=8cm, height=5.5cm,
				xlabel={$t$}, ylabel={$x$},
				axis lines=middle,
				xmin=0, xmax=5, ymin=0, ymax=14,
				xtick=\empty,
				ytick={1}, yticklabels={$x_0$},
				clip=false,
				samples=80,
			]
			% Parabola x(t) = 0.5*t^2 + 1*t + 1
			\addplot[thick, black, domain=0:4.5] {0.5*x^2 + x + 1};
			% Mark x_0
			\filldraw[black] (axis cs:0,1) circle (2pt);
			% Tangent line at t=2 to show v(t), slope = a*t + v_0 = 2+1=3
			\addplot[dashed, black, domain=0.8:3.2] {3*(x-2) + 5};
			\node[right] at (axis cs:3.2,8.6) {slope $= v(t)$};
			\end{axis}
		\end{tikzpicture}
		\end{center}
		If we want to find the velocity at a specific time $t$, we compute the change in position from $t-\delta$ to $t+\delta$ over a period of $2\delta$:
		 \[v(t)\approx \frac{x(t+\delta)-x(t-\delta)}{2\delta}\]
		 \[v(t)\approx \frac{\left(\frac{1}{2}a(t+\delta)^2 + v_0(t+\delta) + x_0\right)-
		 				 \left(\frac{1}{2}a(t-\delta)^2 + v_0(t-\delta)+x_0\right)}{2\delta}\]
	 	Combining like terms:
	 	\[v(t)\approx \frac{\frac{1}{2}a\left[(t+\delta)^2-
	 		(t-\delta)^2\right] + v_0\left[(t+\delta)-(t-\delta)\right]+\left[x_0-x_0\right]}{2\delta}\]
		Using the difference of squares $(t+\delta)^2 - (t-\delta)^2 = (2t)(2\delta)$:
		 \[v(t) \approx \frac{\frac{1}{2}a(2t)(2\delta) + v_0(2\delta)}{2\delta}\]
		\[v(t) \approx at+v_0\]
		Notice that the value of $\delta$ cancels out entirely. Whether $\delta = 10^{-99}$ or $\delta = 10^{99}$, the result is the same. Therefore:
		\[v(t) = at+v_0\]
		And since this graph is linear, the acceleration is:
		\[a(t) = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} = a\]
		
		We will gain a deeper understanding of more complicated formulas in Work and Energy. To summarize the relationships using integral notation:
		\[x(t) = \int v(t)\,dt + x_0\]
		\[v(t) = \frac{dx}{dt} = \int a(t)\,dt + v_0\]
		\[a(t) = \frac{dv}{dt} = \text{constant}\]

\subsection{Kinematics in Vectors}
If the problem requires multiple dimensions, the scalar formulas generalize as follows:
\[\vec{a} = 
\begin{bmatrix}
	a_1\\
	a_2
\end{bmatrix}\]
\[\vec{v} = 
\begin{bmatrix}
	a_1 \\
	a_2 
\end{bmatrix}
t+
\begin{bmatrix}
	v_{1,0}\\
	v_{2,0}
\end{bmatrix}
\]
\[\vec{x} = 
\frac{1}{2}
\begin{bmatrix}
	a_1 \\
	a_2 
\end{bmatrix}
t^2+
\begin{bmatrix}
	v_{1,0}\\
	v_{2,0}
\end{bmatrix}
t+
\begin{bmatrix}
	x_{1,0}\\
	x_{2,0}
\end{bmatrix}
\]
That is, each component obeys the same kinematic equations independently.
